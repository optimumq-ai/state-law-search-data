# Objective

Research one state and identify explicit operational rules governing municipal public-records processing that could ultimately become configurable options within a public-records management platform.

This is an exploratory research exercise intended to:

* identify legal and procedural concepts that may need to be configurable;
* identify possible values, classifications, deadlines, permissions, prohibitions, exceptions, triggers, notices, and procedural events associated with those concepts;
* preserve authoritative source language and direct official source links;
* distinguish generally applicable law from agency-specific practices;
* distinguish request-processing rules from record-specific exemptions and retention requirements;
* reveal additional concepts that should be considered in later states;
* help determine the appropriate structure and scope of a future multistate configuration library.

This exercise is not intended to:

* determine whether a municipality is legally compliant;
* provide legal advice;
* resolve ambiguous legal questions;
* decide whether a particular real-world record must be disclosed;
* predict how a court would decide a dispute;
* design the final software configuration structure;
* normalize rules across all states;
* create a final national taxonomy;
* produce an exhaustive inventory of every confidentiality statute in the state.

The purpose is to discover and document clearly supported operational rules so the results can later be reviewed and used to improve the research structure and process.

# Current-Law Verification Requirement

Do not assume that an official handbook, guide, manual, FAQ, training document, or agency webpage contains the most recent version of Arizona law.

Before relying on a secondary official source:

1. identify its publication or revision date;
2. determine the period through which it claims to be current;
3. compare it against the current official statute or rule when reasonably possible;
4. review recent enacted legislation that may have amended the relevant statutory provisions;
5. identify any material gap between the source date and the research date.

At minimum, examine:

* the current official version of Arizona’s public-records statutes;
* official legislative enactments from the most recently completed regular legislative session;
* enacted legislation from the current session when it has been signed or otherwise become law;
* official effective-date information;
* official administrative rules relevant to records access, retention, or destruction.

Do not treat a bill as law merely because it passed one chamber, was enrolled, or appears in a legislative search result.

For each recent enactment relied upon, verify:

* bill or act number;
* enactment status;
* approval or signature date when available;
* effective date;
* statutory sections amended or added;
* whether the official codified statute already incorporates the change.

When a current codified statute and an enacted session law appear inconsistent, identify the issue in Notes and do not silently choose one.

# Case Law Boundary

Do not conduct an exhaustive case-law review.

Arizona public-records law may depend materially on appellate decisions. Include an Arizona appellate decision when:

1. it is available through an official Arizona judicial source;
2. it clearly establishes, limits, or clarifies an operational rule relevant to municipal public-records processing;
3. the rule is not adequately stated in a statute, rule, or formal Attorney General opinion; and
4. the holding can be expressed without predicting how the rule applies to a new factual scenario.

Case law may be used to identify operational concepts such as:

* the meaning of “promptly”;
* burden or best-interest considerations;
* search obligations;
* metadata or electronic-record treatment;
* denial procedures;
* segregability;
* privacy balancing;
* attorney fees;
* administrative burden;
* special-action remedies.

Do not use case law merely to:

* decide whether a specific record is public;
* predict a litigation outcome;
* resolve disputed facts;
* automate a balancing test;
* infer a rule not clearly stated;
* create detailed legal-decision logic.

When a case is included:

* state the narrow operational rule;
* identify whether it is a holding or explanatory language;
* identify important factual or doctrinal limitations;
* avoid quoting headnotes;
* use the official opinion text.
